In Sipolog (From The Bamboo Dancers) by N. V.M. Gonzalez 
 
The master of the Capitan Tiago, a fat little man in his fifties, preferred the camisa de chino to the standard ship captain's uniform, and he liked his dinner served outside his cabin. For this purpose, the steward cleared the dining table and spread at one end a yellow table cloth. On this he set a bottle of beer, a bowl of roasted peanuts, and an empty glass. 
"Anyone joining me here?" the captain boomed. For audience, he had the thirty-odd first-class passengers dozing in their cots and the few who were up and about like me. I had installed Rosa in the third class section, which was far out astern, where she hugged her knees under a grey flannel blanket and dozed. 
"Beer, partner. How about beer?" the master asked me directly. I observed that he addressed just about everybody 
"partner," a concession — as Pepe himself had shown at the airport — to his sense of brotherhood with the whole world. To humor him, 1 joined him at the table, helping myself to some of the peanuts. 
Perhaps he had noticed the hotel stickers on my bags, perhaps he saw it in the gray dacron-cord pants I was wearing, for he said: 
"So, you've just come from 
America, partner?" 
"Oh, well, yes," I admitted. 
"I've been on the ultra mar myself." He gave the name of a freighter, the Santa Monica I think it was, on which he had served as second mate. 
"For how long?" I asked. 
"Five years, partner." 
"And you couldn't stand the long voyages?" 
 
"It was mainly the cold, partner. 
Oh, that winter cold in the high seas," he said. 
"But it must have been an education, I'm sure." "Education? Let me tell you something, partner", , he began excitedly, 
as if had unwittingly touched off a subject he was rather sanguine about. "There are only three things in education la cabeza, el corazon, y el otro!" 
The switch to Spanish reminded me of Herb looking at the tinkling dancers and throwing Spain into that lilting music. In the fat little shipmaster's case, I suspected Spain was a professional prop. It set him down as a person of character, whatever that was. 
"It's the combination of the training of head, heart, and the other that really matters. That's why I left the high seas," he continued. "It gave much too much training in the wrong places. My heart and head and the other - yes, the other, especially, was getting too much education, partner." He was loud in a boorish way but it somehow became him. 
"Look, Cap," I said, striking up a familiar tone, "you surely made some money overseas, I'm sure." 
"Did I, you think? To get all that education? It's here where the fortune is, partner. Think of it, I've opened up two ports of call in Sipolog — San Jose and Dias." 
"We live near Dias," I said. 
"You'll remember how Dias was three years back, before this ship began to stop there regularly. It's a changed town now. Honestly, partner, 1 think Im responsible for the change — the progress." 
"You surely must have brought progress to Dias," I said to humor him even more. 
 
"For the extra business," I asked, "does the company pay you extra too?" "Ten percent, partner," he said. 
By this time his dinner was ready. 
The cabin boy set it elaborately for him 
- with the ketchup bottle, salt, and pepper shakers and a toothpick-container. 
How I managed to leave him to his steak and fried fish and bananas, I don't know. But I succeeded in the enterprise somehow, and before he got around to his dessert — the cabin boy brought a can of guava jelly, too — he was expounding on "the heart, the head, and the other" to yet another captive listener. 
There is a pleasant and steady vibration that engines make and a thythmic wash of the water over the ship's side that invites sleep. To me, these make sea voyages extremely delightful. I had seven hours of something like near-bliss, every minute a soothing balm to the nerves. I slept a good half of that time, waking up to another quiet world. Over the ship's side the floodlights revealed a pier. It was past midnight, and we had anchored at Talim harbor. 
By eight the next morning we reached San Roque, the main port of Sipolog. The voyage from Talim had been as smooth as that from Manila, but Rosa had got seasick and now looked pale and weak. She would not eat the breakfast served by the ship's mess for third-class passengers, which consisted of dried fish, rice, and coffee. I hoped she was not thinking that she should have gone by bus in the first place. "You would rather take the bus to Dias from here?" I asked. 
Two red buses were waiting for passengers at the far rump of the pier. 
"You'll reach Dias in six hours. 
From Dias, you can walk two kilometers down the beach to get home," 
 
This suggestion must have redeemed me in her estimation. She hastily gathered her things and followed me down the companion way to the lower deck and down the gangplank, and then we walked past huge piles of copra and lumber laid out on the dock. Outside the pier enclosure were several makeshift restaurants, and we stopped at the first one on the row. There I bought Rosa a cup of coffee and some bread "You'll be hungry if you don't eat," I said, also ordering a cup of coffee for myself. 
Then we walked over to the bus. I bought her a ticket and saw her safely installed in one of the forward seats. By six that evening she'd be home. The Capitan Tiago, though, would not get to Dias before ten that evening. She would certainly get home ahead of me. "You'll be all right." I told Rosa. 
"Ask Mother to send someone over to meet me with a horse." 
The other bus passengers gave me a queer look as I spoke to her, or so I felt. 
It was a long time since I had treated, or spoken to anyone, with such kindness. 
"Just see that you don't lose your ticket," I said with a little gruffness now, which was more like how I wanted to sound. The horse materialized that evening from out the darkness. It was a wet, sticky darkness. 
Rain met the ship as she swung out beyond Dumali Point and never quite left us from then on. Three hours later, though it had considerably lessened, you could still see the rain quite clearly in the floodlights hung over the ship's side to provide illumination for the stevedores and their dinghies. 
The rain was stringy like confetti, I thought, sitting on top a heap of cargo that had been piled on to the dinghy. The light from the ship followed us a short distance, 
 
and looking back I saw the master at the bridge gazing at the area and the sound of little waves on the shore grew distinct, and indeed louder, as we approached. Lanterns hung ahead, and the dinghy struck gravel, and people were jumping off and wading waist deep in the water. "Idrus!" I shouted, recognizing one of the men with the lanterns. "Have you brought a horse?" He had been working as a farm hand with us for as far back as I could remember. How I got to dry land I don't know. 
People crowded all around the little boat, which rocked with the breakers pounding down the length of the shore and bounced each time its keel struck bottom. I held on to my fishing-rod case, which, I 
•thought, could very well get lost or get broken under the circumstances. And then somebody offered to carry me off across the water on to dry ground. There was Idrus waiting, holding the lantern high up over his head. In a minute, he had brought my bag and loaded it on to a carabao sled. All this he did while he held up that lantern with one hand and I could see his legs and as well as his head and shoulders. He was bowlegged and had spread-out toes. His face was that of a happy boy. 
"Are you ready?" he said 
"Where's the horse?" 
"A few hundred yards from here." 
"Did Rosa get home safely?" "She's probably done with her supper now," Idrus assured me. 
The sled runners grated in the sand. 
With Idrus on its back, the carabao lumbered along, a black hulk in the darkness so that it was as if a huge lump of darkness itself were moving. To our right, two miles across the water, the Capitan Tiago rode at anchor, like a castleagainst the sky where a great feast or a wedding was in progress. 
 
Either the horse knew me (I was vain enough to think so or it was just plain spirited, but no sooner had i mounted the saddle than it broke into a gallop. Idrus shouted. "Be careful!" - and the horse became more wild. "There's the mangrove swamp ahead, remember!" Idrus shouted at the top of his voice. 
Pebbles scattered all along the way; I could hear them fall like hailstones on corrugated iron roofing. Before long I was in cold sweat and, in my panic, I let the reins loose. This made the horse run faster, breaking twigs and slashing over pandan leaves. 
And I had not become too much a stranger not to know it either when the horse came to the stand of mangrove trees. 
They were dead trees, 1 remembered now. 
Gnarled trunks and branches strewn all over all along the beach, 1 remembered. 
The horse now and then knocked its knee, stumbled, struggling through the soft mud and thus breaking the rhythm of its gallop. 
Theld on to its mane, which was long and thick. Bending low that.way I avoided the branches overhead. The horse, too, sweated profusely and I could feel my calves moist, the sweat having soddened my dacron-cord pants. 
We came to the pebbled road that led to the coconut grove and now the horse switched on to an easy trot. A light shimmered through the leaves and I tried to check the horse, but it broke into another gallop instead, certain that the house was just a little way ahead. It was all I could do to avoid falling. 
I slipped off the saddle at last in order to lead the horse through the narrow gate. Someone was holding up a lantem so 1 could see my way. 
 
It was Father. The lantern light fell on his face, and it was an old man's face. 
All through my days in America in a hundred old men's faces 1 had met in parks, restaurants, and other places, 1 had seen Father's face. That was perhaps the reason why I had dreaded meeting those old men and old women. Now the meaning of that fear was clear. 
The lantern light was not too bright; the resin-yellow flame flickered in the thick and fat and sooty chimney that enclosed it. But behind that light, a little to one side, was Father's face. He held the lantern steadily, and he looked happy. 
He was pleased with the Borsalino, which I had brought along to give him as my homecoming present, a pasalubong as we say in Tagalog. He tried it on, and I explained to him its virtues - that it was of the finest felt and that it couldn't possibly get out of shape. It fitted him wonderfully. 
But he'd like to keep it, if I didn't mind, he said, to wear in the future 
- say, during the coming town fiesta in Dias. I wanted to tell how I had worn the hat on my way to San Francisco, but he did not seem interested in the story I had in mind. He was bothered about Pepe. 
"But he will come," he said, tapping the ground with the bamboo stick he carried. 
"I know Pepe, and I'm sure he will come." 
He had been showing me his poultry house and kitchen garden. A civet had run off with a hen the night before, and he showed me the place where the creature had slipped through the reed fence. And, worse, Japanese giant snails plagued his string beans, he said. But he was not losing heart; no, not in the least. 
 
"If you stay here, do your work here," he said, "you might help me collect a pailful of those snails every morning. Remember, they were not here before the war. Now they're all over the country, I hear." 
"But Papa," 1 protested. "T11 rot here!" 
"Then go where your work is, but don't forget that your home's here," he said. 
I could say nothing to that, and I kept quiet thereafter. You learn from old folks in due course many things you couldn't possibly argue about. When I left them up in the house, Mother and Gaya had been discussing Pepe. Since Pepe's Singapore trip, Mother and Gaya had lived together. They were the exception to the general rule that mothers-in-law could never live under the same roof with their daughters -in-law and keep peace. Mother and Gaya were as thick as thieves. This, too, I was learning to accept. 
"Let me show you the place where two men from I don't know which part of the country dug out two pits," Father was saying.
"What for?" 
"They claimed there's buried treasure here," Father said, picking up his bamboo stick and leading the way. We left the back lot behind the kitchen garden and then walked through a thick stand of cogon grass which at this time of the year had long cottony flowers streaking white in the sun. You'd think that squat brown soldiers wearing shakos were marching toward us. 
"That's possible, Sipolog used to be the hide-out of pirates," I said. "Historians say so, at least." 
"Quite so," Father said. "And as I've told you, the two men were here and offered to sign an agreement to give us half of the treasure if they found any." 
 
"They found you aren't the man they can swindle, I know." 
"Not quite so," Father said. "l judged they were serious and honest." "Did they find any treasure then? 
But, of course, they didn't!" I laughed. "Actually, they were feeling their way around." 
"What can they get from an old man like me?" Father laughed, too. 
We were walking now toward a tall narra tree. There had been two of those before, I remembered from way back as a boy attending school in Dias and the family had just moved in from town to live on the farm. The holes, two of them, were some five meters apart; forming the base of a triangle with the surviving narra tree at the other end. Those treasure-hunters were perhaps honorable enough men, granted; but that didn't mean, I said to myself, that they could be right in their guess. I was sure now that they told Father some complicated story about a dying inmate in Bilibid or some such prison leaving them a map and begging that a portion of the treasure be used to make possible some Count of Monte Cristo plot. 
They had gone four feet deep into the sandy loam at each pit. If it was true that wicked other-world creatures lived in trees, he who had made the narra his home must have surely split his side laughing. 
Father had the other tree felled some years back and had got from it, I know, the timber for the walls and floors of the house; but he had left the stump on the ground. Possibly, I could do something with it, since the wood was good. Looking it over now, I must have revealed my interest only too clearly. Father said: "You know I saved that for you." 
"Just now I don't know what to do 
with it," I said.
 
"Then let it stay. Perhaps in the future..." 
"Can you imagine Pepe suggesting," 
I said, "that 1 do something about driftwood?" 
"Just like Pepe," Father said. "He has never been imaginative enough, really." "But you think he'll come," I said. "I hope he'd even set up his practice here." 
"And get his fees in rice and fish?" 
"What of it?" 
"It will be quite a sacrifice." 
"He has to make a decision," Father said. "I'll only suggest it. He'll have to make up his own mind himself." 
It was Pepe who had warned me about how rampant dynamite-fishing had become here at home, but so far I had not yet heard any explosions, nor had anyone minus an arm or a leg been carried to Dias in a hammock. "But are the tarpons back?" I asked Idrus. 
He had shown me a trail I had not used before and which led to the Alag River. had got there in less than twenty minutes and caught nothing, although I had spent some four hours trying out my favorite spots. 
"The dynamiters have finished them, Idrus," I said. 
"Oh, no," he smiled. "It's only because the moon's not quite right for tarpon-fishing," he said. "You might try the rocks off the Point tomorrow. 
Shall I have the banca ready for you?" 
"Why don't you come?" I said. He could finish with his chores early and it would be really good to have him. Well, he'd see, he had promised; but we had to put off going together because Pepe arrived that afternoon. 
 
He couldn't stand it alone in the city and so had locked up the house and left his Plymouth at a garage, I suspected. 
Then he had taken the bus to Batangan, just as Rosa would have done all by herself had I not decided to take her on the boat with me. From Batangan, Pepe had boarded the ferry to San Roque, where the red buses operated by the Sipolog Transportation Co., or the Sitrancos, as people had learned to call them, got him straight to Dias in one piece. Strange that when I had put Rosa on one of the Sitrancos I had never thought of the reckless driving and the bad roads and the rotting wooden bridges along the route; and seeing that Pepe had risked these hazards made me feel somewhat guilty. 
Here he was now toting a plaid bag with a shoulder strap, a portable radio and his doctor's kit. It was Gaya who saw him coming up the bamboo gate from the mangrove-lined beach and she screamed in unashamed joy, forgiving him all his indiscretions then and there; and the whole household ran out to meet him, Father getting there ahead of all of us in spite of his bad leg because he happened to be in the coconut grove picking up nuts 
- that had fallen to the ground. 
Pepe had brought me some mail - a card from Helen postmarked Hong Kong, a mimeographed news sheet from the Rices prepared in lieu of a Christmas card which presented in witty detail the several short trips across America they had made since summer; also a card from Mrs. Ishikawa. But my mind was on Pepe and Gaya, and I was glad for them both now, and happy, even; and not wishing to be in their way I joined Father in the coconut grove. 
He was setting fire to a pile of dried coconut leaves he had put together under 
 
a tree. From afar came the squeak of a water pump that Idrus worked diligently, and I heard Mother shouting: 
"Hurry, Idrus! Hurry! After that long journey, your manong Pepe will want to have a good bath!" as if she wanted the whole world to hear about Pepe's taking a bath. 
Afterward she went past us in her wooden shoes, with Rosa carrying a basket under her arm. "They'll go to see the neighbors who live on the other side of the Point, to see what food they can get and buy and put together for supper," Father said. 
After I had gone about a hundred yards off the rocks, I saw Mother and Meding walking slowly down the shore, and then later the huts came in full view. 
The words of that Hiroshima road-worker came back to me: "Please tell the people of your country how we suffer ..." like a long forgotten proverb. Perhaps, I said to myself, some thousands of miles away beyond any one's normal range of vision the earth being round, another. 
mushroom cloud had already burst open in the sky... 
The grey huts dissolved into the green haze of the shoreline as, turning my back from them, I paddled away. I would go out to the shoals, I told myself. I could spin-fish where gar and barracuda were plentiful. Father's new smudges under the coconut trees became thin filaments in the sky. 
But I should have had Idrus there with me to warn me of the frisky sea. It was the morning tide rising, no doubt. 
Nothing but that, really. And yet it came with a strong current that bucked my efforts. Once more I got close to the rocks and three-feet waves tossed the boat. It was all I could do to steer clear off the Point, and I'd discovered that I could do this by keeping the grey hut on the shore 
 
directly ahead of me, the little carved prow of the boat pointing at them. 
The closer I got, the bigger the waves became; and one particularly mountain-like rush of water sent the banca head-long into the trough. It very nearly tore the paddle off my grip and I was only about fifty yards from dry land when the boat emerged on the crest of a new wave. I was now completely powerless to control it, and this fact stunned me like a blow on the head. The next trough turned the boat sidewise and sent it see-sawing forward, sucking in my Richie & Sunderland rod. I saw the handle and the screwed on reel slip into the water. My first thought was: Hope it won't break. And the next: Might find some way this afternoon to look for it after the tide's gone down. And by way of an amendment: Idrus could probably dive for it. But the line hung and the hook caught somewhere at the edge of the boat, and this held the line in such a taut it could almost sing while the rod dangled awkwardly in the water, all the six and one-half feet of it in green fiberglass with chromed guides and gold winding; and then all this took the full force of the next wave. 
I could do nothing but jump into the water, and it was here I made my first serious mistake. I knew it the instant my feet hit the oyster-covered rocks, I could have had sense enough to jump off earlier, in deeper water; and I could have swum with my things to the shore. The waves would have sent me home without the slightest effort. But now I was between getting pinned down by the boat and being lashed at by the outriggers. These had come off after the pounding against the rocks, and waist-deep water absurdly reached up to my neck, and I choked from the boat's weight before me and the hard rind of a bamboo outrigger now at my neck. 
Somehow I got up and skipped out of the water's edge. But I remembered that I should be salvaging my tassle bag if it had not been washed away; and so l hopped back into the trough that was gluey with dry seaweeds and foam. The bag rolled about in the water inside the banca, like a greenish-blue puffer that had long been dead, while the loose outrigger tapped away at the side of the banca what sounded like a sad dirge for me as I stood there on wave-washed ground 
Dripping, the bag fell off my hand once as I staggered out of breath to the coconut grove. Pepe's portable radio was playing cool jazz and the wind in the coconut palms rattled away an accompanying buzz by making music with the dry leaves. It was the strangest song I had ever heard. Through the gap in the trees I saw Pepe and Gaya at the porch sitting quietly, fulfilled at last in their long-sought-for reunion. I had almost drowned, and yet they had not even seen me. The thought made my blood swell in rage, and I realized I could not keep myself under control however hard I tried. "Look," I said, "I almost died out there!" I shouted as I clambered up the porch steps weakly. "You understand, I almost died!" 
They stared at me, open-mouthed, as if confronted by a stranger speaking a different language; and they didn't even have the politeness to switch off the radio and to ask me why I was all sore and wet, or what it was I was saying. Quite possible, those words never succeeded in escaping my lips. I have not found out the truth, and up till now I've not asked.
